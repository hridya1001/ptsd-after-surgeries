Significance of Journey & Empathy Maps in This Project
How these tools shaped our understanding of the problem and guided the design process.
  • Journey maps allowed us to trace the full arc of the patient experience:from pre-surgery anxiety through post-discharge isolation, making the emotional timeline visible and actionable.
  • The empathy map layers (thinks, feels, says, does) revealed a sharp disconnect between what patients outwardly said ("I'm okay") and what they internally experienced (panic, confusion, abandonment).
  • By mapping emotion scores across stages, we identified critical drop points:particularly during anesthesia and the recovery room where PTSD risk is highest and intervention is most needed.
  • Pain points and opportunities surfaced at each stage directly fed into our "How Might We" questions, ensuring our solutions are grounded in real user experiences rather than assumptions.
  • Comparing two radically different personas (a 19-year-old student vs. a 72-year-old journalist) highlighted how PTSD triggers are universal across age and background, validating the systemic nature of the problem.

How Might We?
Five design questions framed directly from our root cause analysis, following the HMW formula: intended action + potential user + desired outcome.
The "How Might We" Formula
Taught as part of the design thinking methodology at our institution.
Our 5 How Might We Questions
Framed based on the four root cause branches — information gap, sensory environment, anesthesia experience, and post-discharge isolation.

  1. How might we redesign surgical consent for preoperative patients so that they feel psychologically prepared, not just legally consented?

  2. How might we explain what surgery will feel like for patients before their operation so that they don't feel scared and lost when it actually happens?

  3. How might we make the recovery room feel less overwhelming for patients waking up after surgery so that they feel safe instead of panicked?

  4. How might we better match the amount of anesthesia to each person's body for patients undergoing surgery so that no one wakes up or stays conscious during their procedure?

  5. How might we stay in touch with patients after they go home for people recovering from surgery so that they don't feel alone and confused when something feels wrong?

8-3-5 Brainstorming Rule
Based on each How Might We question, we conducted an 8-3-5 brainstorm activity — 8 people, 3 ideas each, 5 rounds — resulting in 192 total solutions.
What is the 8-3-5 Rule?
A structured rapid ideation technique used in design thinking.
  • 8 participants generate ideas simultaneously, removing group-think bias.
  • 3 ideas per person per round, balancing quality and quantity.
  • 5 rounds of idea generation, with sheets passed between participants for cross-pollination of concepts.
  • Applied once per HMW question, yielding 8 × 3 × 5 × (number of questions) = 600 ideation slots, filtered down to 192 distinct solutions.

Top Ideas & Prototypes
The highest-impact ideas selected from 192 brainstormed solutions, developed into three tangible prototypes.
Top 7 Ideas
Shortlisted from the 8-3-5 brainstorm based on feasibility, impact, and alignment with root causes.
1

Give patients a simple one-page visual comic strip explaining their surgery step-by-step in plain language.

2

Give patients a simple breathing exercise card to practise the night before, to use during induction.

3

Give patients a cartoon comic strip showing every step of their surgery day / a simple printed card explaining where the patient is.

4

Play a friendly doctor's recorded voice narrating 'what happens next' in the pre-op bay.

5

Provide a printed discharge summary in plain language the patient can read at home.

6

Design a bold awareness poster campaign for hospital wards, GP waiting rooms, and community centres: 'You Are Not Alone After Surgery — Here's Who to Call.'

7

National poster and infographic campaign across all NHS trusts, GP surgeries, pharmacies: 'Recovery Doesn't Stop at the Hospital Door.'
